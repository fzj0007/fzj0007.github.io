Overview of Blended Learning

o   What is Blended Learning?

§  Taking traditional in-person lessons and enhancing them both in and out of the classroom with technology.

§  NOT a way of teaching hybrid classes.

o   History of blended learning

§  First “distance class” was a series of postcards between teacher and students.

§  Sparsely implemented before COVID.

§  Concepted by many school systems as a way to teach students in-person and at home at the same time.  

§  Rise of COVID money allowed more schools to integrate BL aspects

o   Common problems in implementing Blended Learning

§  General lack of knowledge

§  Used as a replacement rather than a supplement

§  Netflix syndrome- too many options

o   SAMR and TPACK models

§  SAMR

§  Substitution: In this level, technology is considered as a tool substitute with no functional change. This can be done by upgrading or updating tools, while keeping constant the functional use.

§  Augmentation: Technology is described as a tool substitute with some functional improvements. Additional uses or benefits of the technology are explored and applied in this level.

§  Modification: Technology is a tool that allows for significant redesign of lessons. According to this level, the tool is used not just for performing the same functional task, but to modify or redesign parts of the whole activity to transform and enhance student learning. For example, using a voice to text application, students with limited physical ability can still engage in the process of writing.

§  Redefinition:  Technology is used to create new tasks and activities which were previously inconceivable within a traditional classroom. It allows us to reimagine and revitalize the learning process in the classroom.

§  https://sites.google.com/a/msad60.org/technology-is-learning/samr-model

§  TPACK

§  Pedagogical Content Knowledge (PCK) - knowledge that teachers have about their content and the knowledge that they have about how teach that specific content.  This specialized knowledge allows teachers to use the most effective methods for teaching specific content.

§  Technological Pedagogical Knowledge (TPK) - the set of skills which teachers develop to identify the best technology to support a particular pedagogical approach. For instance, if you want your students to work in collaborative groups (pedagogy) you might choose to have them share their learning in a wiki (a digital tool that is collaborative) or communicate what they have learned in a multimodal presentation.

§  Technological Content Knowledge (TCK)- the set of skills which teachers acquire to help identify the best technologies to support their students as they learn content.

§  https://www.powerschool.com/blog/the-tpack-framework-explained-with-classroom-examples/

 

o   Easy ways to integrate blended learning into the classroom

§  Discussion Posts

·      Discussion posts allow students an easy avenue for collaboration.

·      Posts can be used before, during, or after a lesson.

·      Teachers can post discussions to activate student prior knowledge before a lesson, use as a checkpoint during a self-guided lesson, or as an “exit ticket” at the end of the lesson.

·      Not all discussions have to be in class! If students have access to technology and internet outside of the classroom, teachers can extend the collaboration beyond the walls of the classroom.

·      Discussions can engage all learners: most LMS offer ways to encourage students to post to the discussions before being able to complete subsequent tasks.

§  Assessments

·      Substituting paper tests for online assessments.

·      LMS provides an easier way to more assess more frequently- short quizzes, polls, etc.

·      LMS provide ways to create higher order thinking questions that can be automatically assessed.

§  Creating an online community

·      Your online “classroom” bay be an extension of the physical classroom, but classroom management strategies may not transfer to your online environment.

·      You must teach your students online classroom etiquette, or they may behave like how they do on their online games.

·      Create an online “Code of Conduct.”

·      Examples:

o   Using “I” statements when disagreeing with a discussion post.

o   Addressing others by name when making comments in a discussion thread.

o   Only attaching educationally appropriate materials.

§  Which one is right for you? Things to consider:

·      What does you school use?

o   Are you in an Apple environment? Chromebook/Google environment? Microsoft? Choose one that will benefit you the most!

o   What LMS does your school use? What works with your LMS? How do you access your email? Some LMS work with services better than others; however, if you are a Microsoft school, using Google products (Slides, Forms, etc.) may not be endorsed by your school.

·      All about Apple!

·      Best if you are in an all-Apple environment.

·      Does not offer services that integrate with LMS; however, does offer services that are beneficial if you are in an all-Apple 1:1 environment.

·      Great if you are working with Office products (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, OneDrive, etc.)

·      Most compatible with LMS.

·      Easiest to use and very reliable.

·      Not widely supported by schools.

§  Video Projection

·      Considerations:

o   How large is your projection surface? Larger surfaces would warrant a higher quality projector.

o   How far away are you placing the projector from the projection surface? If you are tight on space, consider a “short-throw” projector.

o   If you are using a projector, how bright is your class space? Do you plan on projecting with the room lights off? If not, a projector with higher lumens may be necessary.

o   Do you need a projector, or will a large tv or other solution such as a SMART board or Promethean board suffice?

o   How are you going to get the video signal from the source to the display? Does your display support wired or wireless projection? If your display only supports wired connections and you would like the versatility of projecting wirelessly, what equipment could you use? In most cases, an Apple Tv may be your best option if wireless support is not already built in.

§  Audio Amplification

·      Considerations:

o   How large is your class space? This will determine the size of speaker(s) you will need.

o   Would a permanent or portable solution be best? Will you need to use it in multiple locations?

o   How will you get the sound from the source? Wired options will offer higher quality sound, but wireless options will offer more versatility. Most portable speaker solutions include Bluetooth functionality.

o   If you have larger speakers, are they phantom-powered, or do they need to be plugged in? If they are phantom-powered, do you have the appropriate equipment to power them? In these cases, you will also have a mixing board involved- do you have the necessary cables to go from the board to the speakers and the source to the board?

§  Recording Equipment

·      Considerations:

o   Would a portable system fit your needs, or would a system integrated into the classroom be best?

o   Where will the microphones be placed? This will greatly influence the types of microphones needed.

o   How will you access the recordings? Mobile recording devices will usually require an SD card.

o   Will you need to record? If so, the same questions apply. Consider making the purchase for a PTZ (pan, tilt, zoom) camera.
